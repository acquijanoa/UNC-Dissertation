### Abstract

**Background and Motivation.** The Medical Expenditure Panel Survey (MEPS), produced by the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ), is the primary U.S. federal source for individual- and family-level data on health care use, costs, and insurance coverage. MEPS employs a complex stratified, multi-stage cluster probability design with unequal selection probabilities, reflecting deliberate oversampling of minority and low-income subgroups to achieve adequate representation. A well-documented but underaddressed challenge in MEPS analyses is **item-level missingness on annual total out-of-pocket medical expenditure**: approximately 15–25% of surveyed households report no expenditure data in any given panel year, driven by a mechanism that is strongly related to observable covariates—insurance status, health utilization patterns, region, income, and self-reported health status—satisfying a Missing at Random (MAR) assumption conditional on these predictors. The standard response to this missingness in applied health economics is complete-case analysis, which silently drops non-reporting households and computes population estimates only on the observed subsample. This approach is doubly problematic: (i) it discards design information encoded in the survey weights of non-respondents, and (ii) it produces population expenditure estimates that are biased downward, because lower-income households with higher true spending burden are systematically more likely to be missing. The resulting underestimates of catastrophic out-of-pocket spending prevalence skew policy decisions. Furthermore, health economists regularly use these survey data to evaluate the causal effects of policy interventions—such as health insurance coverage or Medicaid eligibility—on out-of-pocket spending, a task severely confounded by self-selection, clinical health status, and complex survey designs.

**Statistical Challenge.** Existing imputation methods for survey data fall into two broad categories. Parametric methods (e.g., predictive mean matching in MICE, linear regression imputation) impose functional form restrictions that are violated by the right-skewed, zero-inflated distribution of health expenditure. Model misspecification in the imputation model introduces bias into downstream population estimates that cannot be corrected by design weighting alone. Nonparametric alternatives such as hot-deck imputation preserve marginal distributions but ignore covariate relationships, producing imputed values that are statistically inefficient and fail to propagate model uncertainty. Critically, **neither class of methods accounts for the complex probability sampling design when fitting the imputation model**, treating the survey sample as if it were an independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) dataset. This ignores stratification and within-cluster correlation structure, leading to a pseudo-posterior distribution for the imputed values that is incorrectly calibrated—specifically, one whose credible intervals are systematically too narrow, underestimating the true uncertainty in population expenditure estimates. No existing nonparametric Bayesian imputation method resolves both of these problems simultaneously.

**Proposed Method: Random Weight Survey BART for Multiple Imputation.** We present **Random Weight Survey BART**, a novel design-consistent multiple imputation framework that resolves both limitations. The method builds on Bayesian Additive Regression Trees (BART; Chipman, George & McCulloch 2010), which model the conditional distribution of the missing outcome as a flexible sum-of-trees:

$$E[Y_i \mid X_i] = \sum_{j=1}^{m} g(X_i; T_j, M_j)$$

where each tree $T_j$ partitions the covariate space and $M_j$ are the associated terminal node parameters. BART's nonparametric architecture requires no assumption about the functional form of the expenditure-covariate relationship, accommodating the complex interactions among income, insurance type, health status, geographic region, and utilization that jointly drive expenditure.

The key methodological innovation is the **injection of Kim, Rao & Wang (2024) bootstrap weights directly into the BART MCMC transition steps**, rather than treating survey weights as fixed post-hoc multipliers. At each MCMC iteration $t$, we draw a fresh bootstrap weight replicate $\mathbf{w}^{*(t)}$ from the Kim-24 stratified multinomial bootstrap:

$$r_{hi}^{*(t)} \sim \text{Multinomial}\!\left(n_h - 1,\ \tfrac{1}{n_h}\mathbf{1}_{n_h}\right), \quad w_{hi}^{*(t)} = \frac{n_h}{n_h - 1} \cdot r_{hi}^{*(t)} \cdot w_{hi}$$

where $h$ indexes strata, $i$ indexes PSUs within stratum $h$, and $w_{hi}$ is the original design weight. This bootstrap resampling simultaneously respects the stratified clustering structure of MEPS (preserving variance estimator consistency) and induces approximately 37% zero-weight "out-of-bag" draws per iteration, which serve as natural held-out validation units for posterior predictive checking during the MCMC run.

These bootstrap weights $\mathbf{w}^{*(t)}$ are injected into three components of the BART sampler at each step:
1. **Metropolis-Hastings tree structure proposals (GROW/PRUNE):** The log-likelihood ratio governing tree acceptance uses the weighted sufficient statistics $\sum_i w_i^{*(t)} (y_i - \hat{f}_{-j}^{(t)}(x_i))^2$ and $\sum_i w_i^{*(t)} (y_i - \hat{f}_{-j}^{(t)}(x_i))$, ensuring that oversampled units in MEPS (e.g., low-income households) do not disproportionately drive tree structure decisions.
2. **Gibbs sampling of terminal node leaf parameters:** Each leaf $\mu_\ell$ is drawn from a design-weighted Gaussian posterior, where the sufficient statistics are accumulated using $w_i^{*(t)}$ rather than unit weights. This ensures that the posterior mean for each tree partition correctly targets the population conditional mean rather than the sample conditional mean.
3. **Gibbs sampling of the residual variance $\sigma^2$:** The weighted residual sum of squares $\text{WRSS}^{(t)} = \sum_i w_i^{*(t)} (y_i - \hat{f}^{(t)}(x_i))^2$ governs the inverse chi-squared posterior draw for $\sigma^2$, calibrating predictive interval width to the design-adjusted residual variance.

The consequence is that each post-burn-in MCMC draw $\hat{y}_j^{(t)}$ for a missing unit $j$ constitutes a **design-consistent imputed value**: it is drawn from a posterior predictive distribution that has been perturbed at every iteration by the sampling design variance, so the resulting credible intervals correctly cover the true conditional mean under the complex design—not just the sample.

**Multiple Imputation, Causal Estimation, and Inference.** The $T$ post-burn-in draws generate $T$ completed datasets. For each completed dataset $t$, the design-weighted Horvitz-Thompson estimator of the population mean annual out-of-pocket expenditure is:

$$\hat{\bar{Y}}^{(t)} = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i \in \mathcal{R}} w_i \, y_i \;+\; \sum_{j \in \mathcal{M}} w_j \, \hat{y}_j^{(t)}}{\displaystyle\sum_{k \in \mathcal{S}} w_k}$$

where $\mathcal{R}$ is the set of respondents, $\mathcal{M}$ is the set of non-respondents, and $\mathcal{S} = \mathcal{R} \cup \mathcal{M}$ is the full sample. We extend this framework to causal inference by estimating the Population Average Treatment Effect (PATE) of a binary exposure $Z$ (e.g., insurance coverage) on expenditure $Y$. For each completed dataset $t$, we compute the design-weighted Augmented Inverse Probability Weighting (AIPW) estimator:

$$\hat{\tau}_{\text{AIPW}}^{(t)} = \frac{1}{\displaystyle\sum_{k \in \mathcal{S}} w_k} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} w_i \left[ \left( \hat{y}_i^{(t)}(1) + \frac{Z_i (y_i^{(t)} - \hat{y}_i^{(t)}(1))}{\hat{\pi}^{(t)}(X_i)} \right) - \left( \hat{y}_i^{(t)}(0) + \frac{(1 - Z_i)(y_i^{(t)} - \hat{y}_i^{(t)}(0))}{1 - \hat{\pi}^{(t)}(X_i)} \right) \right]$$

where $y_i^{(t)}$ is the completed outcome (observed $y_i$ if $i \in \mathcal{R}$, and imputed $\hat{y}_i^{(t)}$ if $i \in \mathcal{M}$), $\hat{y}_i^{(t)}(z)$ is the Survey BART prediction of the potential outcome $Y_i(z)$ for $z \in \{0, 1\}$, and $\hat{\pi}^{(t)}(X_i)$ is the design-consistent propensity score draw. This doubly-robust formulation combines nonparametric outcome modeling with design weighting. The posterior distributions of both $\hat{\bar{Y}}^{(t)}$ and $\hat{\tau}_{\text{AIPW}}^{(t)}$ propagate both model uncertainty and survey design variance directly, avoiding asymptotic sandwich approximations.

**Simulation Validation.** Prior to the MEPS application, we validate the method's properties via a simulation study on 1,000 synthetic survey samples drawn from a known finite population with a stratified two-stage PPS design (30 strata, approximately 170 PSUs), mirroring the MEPS design features. We induce MAR missingness at four levels (5%, 10%, 20%, 50%) using a logistic response model. In addition to testing standard imputation quality (imputation RMSE, population mean bias, and 95% CI coverage), we evaluate the properties of the design-weighted AIPW causal estimator. We measure the bias, RMSE, and empirical coverage of the estimated PATE of a simulated binary treatment under strong confounding, comparing Random Weight Survey BART-AIPW against unweighted BART, complete-case AIPW, and parametric propensity score/outcome regression models.

**MEPS Application.** We apply the method to the MEPS 2021 Full Year Consolidated File, targeting annual total family out-of-pocket expenditure. Respondents are classified as observed or missing based on whether they completed the expenditure diary component. Covariates used in the imputation model include age, sex, race/ethnicity, insurance type and continuity, number of emergency department visits, number of inpatient nights, self-reported health status, chronic condition burden (diabetes, hypertension, asthma, heart disease), region, and metropolitan statistical area classification. We estimate: (i) the **population mean and distribution of annual out-of-pocket expenditure** by insurance status; (ii) the **population prevalence of catastrophic out-of-pocket spending** (defined as exceeding 10% of annual family income); and (iii) the **causal effect (PATE and subgroup CATE) of health insurance coverage** on annual out-of-pocket spending. This analysis demonstrates that after imputing missing expenditures via Survey BART, our design-weighted AIPW estimator corrects for the confounding bias of self-selected coverage, recovering causal impact estimates that are free from both survey selection bias and model misspecification.

**Significance for Biopharmaceutical Practice.** Population-level health expenditure estimates from MEPS are directly used in pharmaceutical cost-effectiveness analyses, budget impact models, and payer negotiation strategies. Biased expenditure and causal treatment effect estimates—arising from naive complete-case analysis or unweighted estimators—propagate into overstated cost-effectiveness ratios, flawed budget impact projections, and miscalibrated health technology assessments. By providing design-consistent multiple imputation and doubly-robust causal inference, the Random Weight Survey BART framework equips health economists with a principled tool to generate valid real-world evidence (RWE) from complex survey data. The method is implemented in open-source Python and integrates naturally with existing survey analysis workflows.
