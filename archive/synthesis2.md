# Synthesis II: Extending Kernel Methods to Complex Survey Data — A Design-Based Kernel Two-Sample Test

## 1. Introduction and Overarching Motivation

Statistical inference from survey microdata underpins some of the most consequential empirical claims in social, health, and economic sciences. The National Health Interview Survey (NHIS), the Medical Expenditure Panel Survey (MEPS), the Current Population Survey (CPS), and the National Longitudinal Survey of Youth (NLSY) collectively inform billions of dollars in federal health, labor, and education policy annually. These datasets share a defining feature: they are generated by **complex probability sampling designs** — stratified, multi-stage, cluster-based mechanisms with unequal selection probabilities — carefully engineered to maximize statistical efficiency subject to cost and logistical constraints.

At the same time, the machine learning community has developed a rich ecosystem of methods for statistical inference from data: two-sample hypothesis tests, distribution shift detectors, generative model evaluators, domain adaptation tools, and fairness auditors. The majority of these methods share a foundational assumption: the data are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) draws from some underlying probability distribution. This assumption is structurally incompatible with how survey data is collected.

The consequences of this mismatch are severe and underappreciated. When machine learning methods are applied naively to survey microdata:
1. **Hypothesis tests are mis-sized:** the null distribution of the test statistic is incorrect under complex sampling, leading to either over-rejection (inflated Type I error) or under-rejection (loss of power) depending on the direction of the design effect.
2. **Distribution estimators are biased:** the empirical distribution of a survey sample does not estimate the population distribution when sampling is informative — units with lower inclusion probabilities are systematically under-represented.
3. **Covariate shift detectors give false alarms:** standard methods will flag distributional shift between two survey waves even when the underlying population distribution is stable, simply because the design features (strata composition, cluster allocation) changed across waves.
4. **Generative model evaluations are invalid:** when synthetic data quality is assessed by comparing synthetic samples to biased survey samples (without weights), the evaluation criterion itself is biased.

This synthesis develops a principled solution to these problems: the **Horvitz–Thompson Maximum Mean Discrepancy (HT-MMD)**, a kernel two-sample test grounded in the design-based empirical process theory of Han and Wellner (2021) and calibrated via the bootstrap weight mechanism of Kim, Rao, and Wang (2024). By embedding the Horvitz–Thompson (HT) empirical process into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), we obtain a general-purpose, nonparametric distributional test that is simultaneously design-consistent, asymptotically valid under complex sampling, and computationally efficient. The framework extends the entire kernel testing paradigm — two-sample tests, goodness-of-fit, independence tests, covariate shift detection — from the i.i.d. setting to the complex survey setting.

## 2. The Machine Learning — Survey Statistics Gap

### 2.1 The Scale of the Problem

The gap between ML methodology and survey data is not a niche concern. The United States government's statistical agencies alone produce over 130 ongoing surveys, many of which are the primary empirical input to academic research in economics, public health, and sociology. Researchers increasingly seek to apply modern ML tools — gradient boosting, neural networks, kernel methods, deep generative models — to these datasets. A 2022 systematic review found that over 60% of applied ML studies using public health survey data (including NHIS, BRFSS, and Medicare claims) did not apply any survey weights in model training or evaluation, and none applied survey-corrected evaluation metrics.

This is not merely a problem of analyst negligence. The problem is structural: the software ecosystems are separate. The `scikit-learn`, `PyTorch`, and `JAX` ecosystems that implement ML methods do not support survey weights natively. The `survey`, `srvyr`, and `survey` R packages that implement survey-weighted estimators do not implement kernel methods. The literatures do not cite each other.

### 2.2 What the ML Community Has and What It Lacks

The ML community has produced powerful distributional testing tools. The **Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD)** (Gretton et al., 2012, *JMLR*) is the canonical kernel two-sample test. It is used to:
- Evaluate the quality of GAN and VAE outputs (Borji, 2019)
- Detect covariate shift in deployment monitoring (Rabanser et al., 2019)
- Measure distributional fairness across demographic groups (Coston et al., 2019)
- Perform hypothesis testing in non-Euclidean spaces (graphs, strings, distributions)

The **kernel Stein discrepancy** (Chwialkowski et al., 2016), **Maximum Mean Discrepancy with importance weights** (Sugiyama et al., 2008), and **Sliced MMD** (Nadjahi et al., 2019) extend the framework in various directions. None of these extensions address the case where the data come from a complex probability sampling design.

Importance-weighted MMD (Sugiyama et al., 2008) is superficially similar but fundamentally different. It addresses **covariate shift** — the case where the source and target distributions differ by a density ratio — and uses density ratio estimation to correct the kernel mean embedding. This assumes the weights are estimated from data and satisfy strong density ratio conditions. It does **not** address the case where the weights are design-based (known, or estimable from the sampling frame), the data come from a finite population, and the inferential target is the finite population distribution rather than a superpopulation density ratio.

### 2.3 What the Survey Statistics Community Has and What It Lacks

The survey statistics community has a deep literature on design-based inference: Horvitz–Thompson estimation, sandwich variance estimators, calibration estimators, and complex bootstrap procedures. Kim et al. (2024) provide the most recent and most general bootstrap calibration framework, extending to quasi-likelihood ratio and quasi-score tests under stratified multi-stage cluster designs.

What the survey statistics literature lacks is **nonparametric distributional testing**. The design-based literature focuses primarily on:
- Point estimation of population means and totals (HT estimator)
- Parametric regression (weighted GLMs, GEEs)
- Categorical table testing (Rao-Scott corrections to chi-squared tests)

There is essentially no design-based analogue of the MMD, the kernel Stein discrepancy, or the energy distance test. When survey researchers want to compare the distribution of an outcome between two subpopulations, they typically resort to t-tests or Wald tests on means — losing the distributional sensitivity that kernel methods provide.

## 3. Background: Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces and the MMD

### 3.1 Kernel Embeddings of Distributions

Let $\mathcal{Y}$ be a measurable space and $k: \mathcal{Y} \times \mathcal{Y} \to \mathbb{R}$ a symmetric, positive-definite kernel. The associated **reproducing kernel Hilbert space** (RKHS) $\mathcal{H}_k$ is the completion of $\{f = \sum_i \alpha_i k(\cdot, y_i)\}$ under the inner product $\langle k(\cdot, x), k(\cdot, y)\rangle_{\mathcal{H}} = k(x,y)$. The **feature map** $\phi: \mathcal{Y} \to \mathcal{H}$ is defined by $\phi(y) = k(\cdot, y)$, and the **kernel mean embedding** of a probability distribution $P$ is:
$$
\mu_P = \mathbb{E}_{Y \sim P}[\phi(Y)] = \int_{\mathcal{Y}} k(\cdot, y) \, dP(y) \in \mathcal{H}
$$
For **characteristic kernels** — including the Gaussian RBF $k(x,y) = \exp(-\|x-y\|^2 / 2\sigma^2)$ and the Matérn class — the map $P \mapsto \mu_P$ is injective. Two distributions are equal if and only if their embeddings are equal: $\mu_P = \mu_Q \Leftrightarrow P = Q$.

### 3.2 The Maximum Mean Discrepancy

The **Maximum Mean Discrepancy** between $P$ and $Q$ is the RKHS distance between their embeddings:
$$
\text{MMD}^2(P, Q) = \|\mu_P - \mu_Q\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 = \mathbb{E}_{x,x' \sim P}[k(x,x')] - 2\,\mathbb{E}_{x \sim P,\, z \sim Q}[k(x,z)] + \mathbb{E}_{z,z' \sim Q}[k(z,z')]
$$
Given i.i.d. samples $\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n \sim P$ and $\{z_l\}_{l=1}^m \sim Q$, the unbiased U-statistic estimator is:
$$
\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_u = \frac{1}{n(n-1)}\!\sum_{i \neq j}\! k(x_i, x_j) - \frac{2}{nm}\!\sum_{i,l}\! k(x_i, z_l) + \frac{1}{m(m-1)}\!\sum_{l \neq r}\! k(z_l, z_r)
$$

Under $H_0: P = Q$, $n \cdot \widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_u \xrightarrow{d} \sum_{j=1}^\infty \lambda_j (Z_j^2 - 1)$, where $\lambda_j$ are the eigenvalues of the integral operator $T_k f(x) = \int k(x,y) f(y) dP(y)$ and $Z_j \stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} N(0,1)$. Because this null distribution is analytically intractable, in practice it is approximated via bootstrap resampling or permutation testing.

**The critical assumption:** the validity of the bootstrap and permutation null approximation rests entirely on the **exchangeability** of the observations under $H_0$. If $P = Q$ and observations are i.i.d., any permutation of the combined sample is equally likely. This exchangeability fails under complex sampling, invalidating both the permutation and the naïve bootstrap.

## 4. Why Standard MMD Fails Under Complex Survey Designs

### 4.1 Bias in the Kernel Mean Embedding

Let $A \subset \mathcal{U}$ be a complex survey sample with inclusion probabilities $\pi_i$, where $\pi_i$ is correlated with the outcome $Y_i$ (informative sampling). The naïve kernel mean embedding estimator is:
$$
\hat{\mu}^{naive} = \frac{1}{|A|} \sum_{i \in A} k(\cdot, Y_i)
$$
Under informative sampling, $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\mu}^{naive}] \neq \mu_P$. Specifically:
$$
\mathbb{E}\left[\hat{\mu}^{naive}\right] = \int k(\cdot, y) \, dP^*(y), \quad \text{where } \frac{dP^*}{dP}(y) \propto \pi(y)
$$
The naïve embedding estimates the **sample distribution** $P^*$, which is tilted toward high-inclusion-probability units. This bias propagates directly into the MMD:
$$
\mathbb{E}\left[\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_{naive}\right] \approx \|\mu_{P^*} - \mu_{Q^*}\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 \neq \|\mu_P - \mu_Q\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2
$$

### 4.2 Incorrect Null Distribution

Even if both $P = Q$ at the population level, the naïve MMD test statistic has a non-zero expected value when sampling is informative, because $P^* \neq Q^*$ even when $P = Q$ (the two samples may have different design features). The permutation test, which pools and re-randomizes the two samples, does not account for the different design structures of the two groups and will spuriously reject $H_0$.

More formally, under informative sampling the HT empirical process has covariance operator:
$$
C^\pi(f,g) = \sum_{i,j \in \mathcal{U}} \frac{\pi_{ij} - \pi_i \pi_j}{\pi_i \pi_j} f(Y_i) g(Y_j)
$$
where $\pi_{ij}$ is the joint inclusion probability. This differs from the i.i.d. covariance operator $C^{iid}(f,g) = \text{Cov}_P(f(Y), g(Y))$ unless the design is simple random sampling. The eigenvalues of $C^\pi$ — which determine the null distribution of $(n+m) \widehat{\text{MMD}}^2$ — are therefore **design-specific**, not distribution-specific. Using the i.i.d. permutation quantiles amounts to using the wrong critical values.

## 5. The HT-Weighted MMD: Construction and Properties

### 5.1 The Design-Unbiased Kernel Mean Embedding

The solution is to apply the Horvitz–Thompson principle directly in the RKHS. The **HT kernel mean embedding** is:
$$
\hat{\mu}_w^P = \frac{1}{\hat{N}} \sum_{i \in A} w_i \, k(\cdot, Y_i), \quad \hat{N} = \sum_{i \in A} w_i \approx N
$$
where $w_i = 1/\pi_i$ are the HT weights. By the HT theorem applied in the RKHS (which is a Banach space with the RKHS norm, so the functional CLT applies):
$$
\mathbb{E}_\pi[\hat{\mu}_w^P] = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{U}} k(\cdot, Y_i) \to \mu_P \quad \text{as } N \to \infty
$$
The estimator is **design-unbiased** for the population kernel mean embedding regardless of whether the sampling is informative.

### 5.2 The HT-MMD Estimator

Given two survey samples $A$ (from $P$ with weights $w_i$) and $B$ (from $Q$ with weights $v_l$), the **HT-MMD** is:
$$
\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_w = \|\hat{\mu}_w^P - \hat{\mu}_w^Q\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 = \frac{\mathbf{w}^\top K_{AA} \mathbf{w}}{\hat{N}^2} - \frac{2\, \mathbf{w}^\top K_{AB} \mathbf{v}}{\hat{N}\hat{M}} + \frac{\mathbf{v}^\top K_{BB} \mathbf{v}}{\hat{M}^2}
$$
where $K_{AA}$, $K_{AB}$, $K_{BB}$ are the three kernel matrices, $\mathbf{w} = (w_i)_{i \in A}$, $\mathbf{v} = (v_l)_{l \in B}$. The **leave-diagonal-out** (unbiased) version excludes $k(Y_i, Y_i)$ terms.

**Design consistency:** Under mild regularity conditions on $k$ (bounded, characteristic) and the sampling design (Poisson, stratified, or rejective sampling with $\pi_i \asymp n/N$):
$$
\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_w \xrightarrow{p} \text{MMD}^2(P, Q) = \|\mu_P - \mu_Q\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2
$$
This follows directly from the Horvitz–Thompson consistency applied to the bounded functional $h(Y_i, Y_j) = k(Y_i, Y_j)$.

### 5.3 Asymptotic Distribution Under $H_0$

By lifting the HT empirical process (Han & Wellner, 2021) into the RKHS, we establish that under $H_0: P = Q$:
$$
(n+m) \cdot \widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_{w,u} \xrightarrow{d} \left\|\mathbb{G}_1^\pi - \mathbb{G}_2^\pi\right\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 = \sum_{j=1}^\infty \lambda_j^\pi \left(Z_j^2 - 1\right)
$$
where $\mathbb{G}_1^\pi, \mathbb{G}_2^\pi$ are independent copies of the HT Gaussian process in $\mathcal{H}$, and $\lambda_j^\pi$ are the eigenvalues of the **HT covariance operator** $C^\pi$. These eigenvalues encode the full design structure — stratification, clustering, and unequal probabilities — into the null distribution of the kernel test. The design effect at the $j$-th eigenvalue is $\text{DEFF}_j = \lambda_j^\pi / \lambda_j$, and the inflation of the test statistic relative to the i.i.d. case is:
$$
\text{DEFF}_{MMD} = \frac{\sum_j \lambda_j^\pi}{\sum_j \lambda_j} = \frac{\text{tr}(C^\pi)}{\text{tr}(C^{iid})}
$$
This is the **kernel-space design effect** — a direct analogue of the classical Kish design effect $\text{DEFF} = 1 + (n-1)\rho_{ICC}$, but defined for the full distributional test rather than a single mean estimate.

## 6. Bootstrap Calibration of the Null Distribution

### 6.1 Kim's Calibrated Bootstrap for the HT-MMD

The null distribution $\sum_j \lambda_j^\pi (Z_j^2 - 1)$ is analytically intractable. Kim et al.'s (2024) bootstrap provides a computationally accessible approximation that consistently estimates the correct eigenvalues $\lambda_j^\pi$.

For each bootstrap replicate $b = 1, \ldots, B$:

**Stage 1 (Design variance calibration):** Apply Kim's multinomial bootstrap to the concave working model (e.g., weighted linear regression) to generate calibrated weight vectors $\{w_i^{*(b)}\}_{i \in A}$ and $\{v_l^{*(b)}\}_{l \in B}$ satisfying:
$$
E_*\{S_w^{par,*}(\hat\theta)\} = S_w^{par}(\hat\theta), \quad V_*\{S_w^{par,*}(\hat\theta)\} = \hat{V}\{S_w^{par}(\hat\theta)\}
$$
These weights encode the design variance (clustering, stratification) without requiring the working model to correctly specify the outcome distribution.

**Stage 2 (Bootstrap HT-MMD):** Compute the bootstrap test statistic using the reweighted kernel matrices:
$$
\widehat{\text{MMD}}^{2,*(b)}_w = \frac{\mathbf{w}^{*(b)\top} K_{AA}\, \mathbf{w}^{*(b)}}{\hat{N}^{*(b)2}} - \frac{2\,\mathbf{w}^{*(b)\top} K_{AB}\, \mathbf{v}^{*(b)}}{\hat{N}^{*(b)}\hat{M}^{*(b)}} + \frac{\mathbf{v}^{*(b)\top} K_{BB}\, \mathbf{v}^{*(b)}}{\hat{M}^{*(b)2}}
$$

**Critical computational efficiency:** The kernel matrices $K_{AA}$, $K_{AB}$, $K_{BB}$ are computed **exactly once** at cost $O((n+m)^2)$. Each bootstrap replicate then requires only matrix-vector multiplications at cost $O((n+m)^2 / B)$ amortized. For large $B = 1000$, the total cost is $O((n+m)^2)$ — identical to a single standard MMD computation.

### 6.2 The Leave-One-Out Connection to U-Statistics

A profound structural insight connects Kim's bootstrap to the U-statistic theory that underlies the MMD. In Kim's bootstrap, the multinomial draw $r^{*(b)} \sim \text{Multinomial}(n-1, \mathbf{1}/n)$ assigns weight zero to each unit with probability $(1-1/n)^{n-1} \approx e^{-1} \approx 0.368$. Approximately $37\%$ of observations are **left out** of each bootstrap replicate.

The MMD's unbiased estimator $\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_u$ is a **second-order U-statistic** of the form $U_n = \binom{n}{2}^{-1} \sum_{i < j} h(X_i, X_j)$. The jackknife variance estimator of a second-order U-statistic is:
$$
\hat{\sigma}^2_{jack} = \frac{4(n-1)}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (U_n^{(-i)} - U_n)^2
$$
where $U_n^{(-i)}$ is the U-statistic with unit $i$ removed. Kim's bootstrap, by setting $w_i^{*(b)} = 0$ for approximately $37\%$ of units in each replicate, generates a **weighted, design-adjusted version of this leave-one-out structure**. When the bootstrap MMD is computed with zero-weight units dropped from the kernel sums, the bootstrap distribution of $\widehat{\text{MMD}}^{2,*}_w$ asymptotically estimates the jackknife variance of the weighted U-statistic — but with the critical addition that the weights $w_i^{*(b)}$ are calibrated to the design moments, so the variance estimate correctly incorporates design-induced clustering and stratification.

### 6.3 Formal Bootstrap Validity

**Theorem:** Under Kim's (2024) regularity conditions on the Stage 1 working model and the Donsker conditions of Han and Wellner (2021) for the kernel class $\{k(\cdot, y): y \in \mathcal{Y}\}$:

1. *(Size control)* Under $H_0: P = Q$, the bootstrap test with critical value $q_{1-\alpha}^* = \text{quantile}_{1-\alpha}\{\widehat{\text{MMD}}^{2,*(b)}_w\}_{b=1}^B$ achieves asymptotically exact size: $P(\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_w > q_{1-\alpha}^*) \to \alpha$.
2. *(Consistency)* Under $H_1: P \neq Q$, $P(\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_w > q_{1-\alpha}^*) \to 1$ as $n, m \to \infty$.

The proof follows the continuous mapping theorem applied to the HT empirical process in $\mathcal{H}$, combined with the bootstrap consistency result of Kim et al. (2024) for the design score moments.

## 7. Extending Machine Learning Methods to Complex Survey Data

### 7.1 Covariate Shift Detection

**The ML problem:** A predictive model trained on data from time $t_1$ is deployed at time $t_2$. Has the input distribution $P(X)$ shifted? Standard covariate shift detectors (e.g., the two-sample MMD on features) assume both datasets are i.i.d. samples.

**The survey version:** Both datasets are waves of a longitudinal complex survey (e.g., MEPS 2018 vs. MEPS 2022). The same complex design was used but cluster compositions and strata may have evolved. A naïve MMD test will falsely detect distributional shift due to design changes even if the superpopulation distribution $P(X)$ is stable.

**The HT-MMD solution:** Apply the HT-MMD comparing the two waves with their respective weights:
$$
H_0: P_{t_1}(X) = P_{t_2}(X) \quad \text{(population-level shift)}
$$
Rejection now indicates genuine population-level change in covariates, not a design artifact.

### 7.2 Domain Adaptation and Transfer Learning

**The ML problem:** Transfer learning from a source domain (e.g., a clinical trial) to a target domain (e.g., a national survey) requires measuring and correcting for distributional shift. The **importance-weighted** approach of Sugiyama et al. (2008) reweights the source sample by the density ratio $\hat{p}_{target}(x) / \hat{p}_{source}(x)$.

**The survey version:** The target domain is a probability sample with known HT weights. The importance ratio is partly known through the design: high-weight units in the survey represent large segments of the population that may be underrepresented in the clinical trial. The HT-MMD provides a principled measure of the distance between the trial distribution and the survey-weighted population distribution, enabling transfer learning corrections that are **design-grounded** rather than estimated entirely from data.

### 7.3 Fairness Auditing Across Demographic Groups

**The ML problem:** A fairness audit tests whether a predictive model produces equivalent outcome distributions across demographic groups (e.g., race, gender). The two-sample MMD on model residuals or predicted probabilities is a natural fairness metric.

**The survey version:** Survey samples are designed to achieve adequate representation of demographic subgroups via stratification — but the within-stratum weights still differ. Applying the naïve MMD to compare Black and White respondents in the NHIS ignores that these groups are sampled at different rates. The HT-MMD produces a test of genuine population-level outcome distribution equality:
$$
H_0: P_{Y|Black}^{population} = P_{Y|White}^{population}
$$
rather than a test of sample distribution equality — a fundamentally different and more meaningful quantity for fairness policy.

### 7.4 Generative Model Evaluation with Survey Data

**The ML problem:** GANs, VAEs, and diffusion models are evaluated by comparing the distribution of generated samples to the distribution of real training data, typically using the MMD or Fréchet Inception Distance (FID). For models trained on survey microdata, this evaluation is flawed if the "real" data comparison uses the biased survey empirical distribution.

**The HT-MMD solution (connecting to Synthesis I):** The DPMM synthetic populations generated in Synthesis I are evaluated against the true population using the HT-MMD:
$$
H_0: P_{DPMM-synthetic}^{population} = P_{real}^{population}
$$
This is the only valid generative model quality metric for survey data, because it compares the synthetic distribution to the **population** distribution (via HT-weighted embeddings) rather than the biased sample distribution.

### 7.5 Anomaly Detection and Surveillance

Population health surveillance systems (e.g., CDC BRFSS, NCHS NHANES) monitor whether health outcome distributions change over time. Traditional surveillance uses simple indicators (prevalence rates, means). The HT-MMD enables **full distributional surveillance**: detecting shifts in the entire outcome distribution, including changes in shape, variance, or tail behavior, that are invisible to mean-based tests.

## 8. Advantages of the HT-MMD Framework

### 8.1 Design Consistency

Unlike importance-weighted MMD methods that estimate density ratios from data (introducing estimation error and model dependence), the HT-MMD uses the **known design weights** $w_i = 1/\pi_i$. These weights are derived from the sampling frame and are design-consistent estimators of the population kernel mean embedding — no density ratio estimation, no bandwidth selection for the weight model, no regularization of the weight estimator.

### 8.2 Nonparametric Distributional Sensitivity

The HT-MMD detects **any distributional difference** between $P$ and $Q$, not just differences in means or variances. For a characteristic kernel (Gaussian RBF, Matérn), the test has power against all alternatives $P \neq Q$. This is a substantial advantage over design-based Wald tests (which test only mean differences) and Rao-Scott corrections to chi-squared tests (which test only categorical marginal distributions).

### 8.3 High-Dimensional and Non-Euclidean Compatibility

The RKHS framework works on any measurable space with a valid kernel. This means the HT-MMD can be applied to:
- **High-dimensional vectors** ($p \gg n$): use the Gaussian RBF or polynomial kernel
- **Functional data** (trajectories, time series): use the functional data kernel on $L^2[0,T]$
- **Graphs and networks** (social networks, biological pathways): use the Weisfeiler-Lehman graph kernel
- **Text and sequences**: use string kernels

Survey data increasingly contains these non-standard data types (GPS trajectories, wearable sensor streams, genetic sequences). The HT-MMD provides a single, unified test applicable across all of them.

### 8.4 Computational Efficiency via Kernel Matrix Reuse

A key practical advantage is that the kernel matrices $K_{AA}$, $K_{AB}$, $K_{BB}$ are computed **once** at cost $O((n+m)^2)$. All $B$ bootstrap replicates are then computed as matrix-vector products, which are highly parallelizable on modern GPUs. For large surveys with $n + m \approx 10{,}000$ and $B = 1{,}000$ bootstrap replicates, the total computation time is comparable to running a single standard MMD test.

Furthermore, kernel approximation methods — **random Fourier features** (Rahimi & Recht, 2007) and the **Nyström approximation** — reduce the $O((n+m)^2)$ kernel matrix to $O((n+m)D)$ where $D \ll n+m$, enabling the HT-MMD to scale to national surveys with $n \approx 100{,}000$ respondents.

### 8.5 Simultaneous Validity of the Two-Stage Decoupling

The two-stage architecture cleanly separates the concavity requirements of Kim's bootstrap (Stage 1, working parametric model) from the nonparametric kernel computation (Stage 2). This decoupling means:
- Kim's regularity conditions (concavity of the quasi-log-likelihood) apply only to Stage 1, where they are satisfied by construction.
- The RKHS kernel computation in Stage 2 is a deterministic function of the data and the bootstrap weights — it is never subject to score-matching conditions or concavity requirements.

The mathematical foundation of this decoupling is the **continuous mapping theorem**: if $w_i^{*(b)} \to w_i$ in distribution (as guaranteed by Kim's bootstrap), and $\text{MMD}^2_w$ is a continuous functional of the weights (which it is, as a bilinear form in the kernel matrices), then $\widehat{\text{MMD}}^{2,*(b)}_w$ consistently estimates the sampling distribution of $\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_w$.

### 8.6 A Unified Dissertation Framework

The HT-MMD connects directly to the DPMM synthetic population proposal (Synthesis I) to create a self-contained, mutually reinforcing dissertation framework:
- **Synthesis I** generates privacy-preserving synthetic populations using the DPMM + Kim bootstrap weights.
- **Synthesis II** evaluates the quality of those synthetic populations using the HT-MMD.
- Both use the same theoretical foundation: the HT empirical process (Han & Wellner, 2021) and Kim's (2024) calibrated bootstrap.
- Both produce the same type of asymptotic guarantee: weak convergence in a function space (RKHS for HT-MMD; $\ell^\infty(\mathcal{F})$ for the DPMM empirical process).

This coherence is a significant advantage for a dissertation: the two methodological contributions are not independent papers but a unified framework for design-correct distributional inference from complex survey data.

## 9. Machine Learning Methods Under Complex Survey Designs: A Systematic Review

The HT-MMD framework introduced in this synthesis is not an isolated method — it is the distributional testing component of a broader program to embed survey-weighting into the full ML pipeline. This section systematically reviews how each major class of ML method can be extended to complex survey designs, identifying the gaps that the HT empirical process framework fills in each case.

### 9.1 Distributional Random Forests

**The i.i.d. method.** Distributional Random Forests (DRF; Cevid, Michel, Näf, Bühlmann & Meinshausen, 2022, *JRSS-B*) extend the standard random forest from estimating conditional means $E[Y | X=x]$ to estimating full conditional distributions $P(Y | X=x)$. The key insight is that a random forest defines a **data-adaptive kernel**: the forest weight $w_i(x) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B \frac{\mathbf{1}[X_i \in L_b(x)]}{|L_b(x)|}$ counts how often training point $i$ falls in the same leaf as the query point $x$. These weights define a local kernel mean embedding:
$$
\hat{\mu}_{DRF}(x) = \sum_{i=1}^n w_i(x)\, k(\cdot, Y_i)
$$
Any distributional quantity (quantiles, CDFs, distributional treatment effects) can be estimated from this kernel embedding.

**The survey extension.** Under complex sampling, the DRF forest weights must be combined with the HT survey weights. The **survey-weighted DRF** uses the composite weights $\tilde{w}_i(x) \propto w_i^{DRF}(x) \cdot w_i^{HT}$, where $w_i^{HT} = 1/\pi_i$ are the Horvitz–Thompson weights. The splitting criterion is modified to the weighted Gini impurity:
$$
\Delta_{split} = \left\|\hat{\mu}_{DRF,w}(x_{left}) - \hat{\mu}_{DRF,w}(x_{right})\right\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2
$$
where each node's embedding is computed using the HT-weighted kernel mean. The HT-MMD directly enters as the node-splitting criterion: the forest partitions the covariate space to maximize the HT-MMD between child node distributions.

**The gap.** No existing DRF implementation supports complex survey weights. The `drf` R package uses unweighted splitting. The survey-weighted DRF requires both (a) modified node splitting criteria and (b) a design-valid uncertainty quantification for the estimated conditional distribution. The HT empirical process theory provides (b): the asymptotic distribution of the leaf-level kernel mean embedding inherits the HT Gaussian process structure, enabling design-valid confidence bands for conditional distributional estimates.

**Application to complex surveys.** A survey-weighted DRF enables estimation of heterogeneous distributional treatment effects in observational survey data — for example, estimating how the full income distribution $P(Income | Race = r, X = x)$ varies across racial groups after conditioning on socioeconomic covariates in the NHIS, accounting for the complex stratified design.

### 9.2 Survey-Weighted Support Vector Machines

**The i.i.d. method.** The Support Vector Machine (SVM; Cortes & Vapnik, 1995) solves a classification problem by finding the maximum-margin hyperplane in the RKHS:
$$
\min_{f \in \mathcal{H},\, b,\, \xi} \frac{1}{2}\|f\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 + C \sum_{i=1}^n \xi_i \quad \text{s.t.} \quad y_i (\langle f, \phi(x_i)\rangle_{\mathcal{H}} + b) \ge 1 - \xi_i,\; \xi_i \ge 0
$$
The dual problem reveals that the SVM solution is a weighted kernel mean: $f = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i k(\cdot, x_i)$ where $\alpha_i$ are the support vector coefficients.

**The survey extension.** The **HT-weighted SVM** replaces the uniform penalty $C$ with a unit-specific penalty $C_i = C \cdot d_i$, where $d_i = w_i / \bar{w}$ are the normalized HT weights:
$$
\min_{f \in \mathcal{H},\, b,\, \xi} \frac{1}{2}\|f\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 + C \sum_{i \in A} d_i \xi_i \quad \text{s.t.} \quad y_i (\langle f, \phi(x_i)\rangle_{\mathcal{H}} + b) \ge 1 - \xi_i
$$
This is equivalent to minimizing the **design-weighted empirical risk** in the RKHS. The dual variables are now bounded as $0 \le \alpha_i \le C d_i$, meaning high-weight (undersampled) units can be more heavily penalized for misclassification. The resulting classifier targets the **population** decision boundary rather than the sample decision boundary.

**Asymptotic validity.** The consistency of the survey-weighted SVM classifier follows from the Rademacher complexity bound applied to the HT empirical process in the RKHS. Specifically, the excess risk of the weighted SVM over the Bayes-optimal classifier converges to zero at rate $O(n^{-1/2})$ under the HT process CLT, matching the i.i.d. rate.

**The kernel trick under complex designs.** A critical observation is that the weighted SVM objective — like the HT-MMD — reduces to a reweighted kernel matrix computation. The Gram matrix entry becomes $\tilde{K}_{ij} = d_i^{1/2} K_{ij} d_j^{1/2}$, where $K_{ij} = k(x_i, x_j)$. This means the entire SVM machinery (kernel trick, efficient quadratic programming solvers, soft-margin classifiers) carries over to the survey-weighted setting with only a change to the weight matrix — a computationally trivial modification.

**Application.** Classification in survey data (e.g., predicting health insurance status from NHIS covariates) with the guarantee that the classifier minimizes the population misclassification rate, not the sample misclassification rate. This is essential for policy applications where the model will be deployed population-wide, not just on the sample.

### 9.3 Kernel Ridge Regression and Gaussian Processes

**The survey-weighted kernel ridge regression (KRR)** solves:
$$
\hat{f}_{KRR,w} = \arg\min_{f \in \mathcal{H}} \frac{1}{\hat{N}} \sum_{i \in A} w_i (Y_i - f(X_i))^2 + \lambda \|f\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2
$$
The closed-form solution is $\hat{f}_{KRR,w}(x) = k_x^\top (\tilde{K} + \lambda W^{-1})^{-1} \tilde{Y}$, where $\tilde{K} = W^{1/2} K W^{1/2}$ is the weighted kernel matrix and $W = \text{diag}(w_i/\hat{N})$.

**Design consistency.** Under informative sampling, $\hat{f}_{KRR,w}$ consistently estimates the population regression function $f^*(x) = E_P[Y | X = x]$, while the naïve unweighted KRR estimates the sample-biased function $E_{P^*}[Y | X = x] \neq f^*(x)$.

**Gaussian processes with survey weights.** The Gaussian process (GP) regression analogue places a prior $f \sim GP(0, k)$ and updates to the posterior given the survey-weighted likelihood:
$$
p(f | \mathbf{Y}, \mathbf{w}) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2\hat{N}} \sum_{i \in A} w_i (Y_i - f(X_i))^2\right) p(f)
$$
This is structurally identical to the DPMM pseudo-posterior from Synthesis I but in the GP regression setting. The Savitsky & Toth (2016) consistency results apply: the pseudo-posterior contracts around the true $f^*$ under informative sampling. The Williams & Savitsky (2021) covariance adjustment (scale-and-shape projection) corrects the posterior credible bands to achieve nominal frequentist coverage under the complex design.

### 9.4 Gradient Boosting and Tree-Based Methods

**Survey-weighted gradient boosting.** Gradient boosting (Friedman, 2001; XGBoost, LightGBM) minimizes a loss function by iteratively fitting trees to the negative gradient. Under complex surveys, the loss function becomes the design-weighted empirical risk:
$$
\hat{F} = \arg\min_F \frac{1}{\hat{N}} \sum_{i \in A} w_i \ell(Y_i, F(X_i))
$$
The gradient at iteration $m$ is $\tilde{r}_i^{(m)} = -w_i \frac{\partial \ell}{\ partial F}\big|_{F=\hat{F}^{(m-1)}}$, i.e., the residual weighted by $w_i$. Both XGBoost and LightGBM support `sample_weight` parameters that implement this modification.

**The critical gap: variance estimation.** The primary limitation of existing implementations is that `sample_weight` in XGBoost addresses the bias of the tree predictor but does **not** correct the uncertainty quantification. Confidence intervals from survey-weighted XGBoost are computed assuming i.i.d. errors, ignoring clustering and stratification. This leads to the same covariance mismatch identified in Synthesis I (§5.2): the model uncertainty is captured but the design uncertainty is ignored.

**The HT-MMD solution.** The distributional test provides a model-free diagnostic: after fitting a survey-weighted XGBoost classifier, apply the HT-MMD to compare the distribution of residuals $Y_i - \hat{F}(X_i)$ between any two subgroups. If the HT-MMD is significantly nonzero, the model has systematic group-level prediction errors that are invisible to the mean-based RMSE metric. This positions the HT-MMD as a **post-hoc model diagnostic** for any survey-weighted ML method.

### 9.5 Neural Networks and Deep Learning

**Weighted empirical risk minimization.** Neural networks trained on survey data should minimize the HT-weighted cross-entropy or MSE loss:
$$
\hat\theta = \arg\min_\theta \frac{1}{\hat{N}} \sum_{i \in A} w_i \ell(Y_i, f_\theta(X_i))
$$
In mini-batch stochastic gradient descent, this requires that each mini-batch is **stratified by design** (sampling mini-batches proportional to HT weights) rather than uniformly at random. Uniform mini-batching re-introduces sample distribution bias: high-weight units appear in mini-batches at their sample rate $1/n$, but should appear at rate proportional to $w_i/\hat{N}$.

**The cluster correlation problem.** The most serious obstacle for deep learning on survey data is that units within the same cluster (PSU) are correlated. Standard mini-batch SGD treats all units as independent, computing gradients that underestimate the true parameter variance. The appropriate gradient estimator is the **cluster-robust mini-batch gradient**:
$$
\hat{g}_{SGD}^{\pi} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}} w_i \nabla_\theta \ell(Y_i, f_\theta(X_i))
$$
where $\mathcal{B}$ is sampled at the **cluster level** (entire clusters are included or excluded), not the unit level. This is the exact analogue of Kim's bootstrap mechanism but applied to each SGD step.

**Double descent under complex designs.** The recent double descent phenomenon in deep learning — where test error decreases again after the interpolation threshold — has been studied only under i.i.d. assumptions. Under complex designs, the effective sample size for neural network generalization is $n_{eff} = n / \text{DEFF}_{NN}$, where $\text{DEFF}_{NN}$ is the neural network design effect (the ratio of design-based generalization error to i.i.d. generalization error). The HT-MMD provides a model-free estimate of $\text{DEFF}_{NN}$ by comparing the distribution of neural network predictions on bootstrapped vs. original survey samples.

## 10. Distributional Weight Calibration via Kernel Moment Matching

The HT-MMD framework does more than test distributional hypotheses — it provides the **objective function** for a new generation of weight calibration procedures. Classical calibration (Deville & Särndal, 1992) adjusts design weights to match known population totals for a set of auxiliary variables. This synthesis proposes extending calibration from moment matching (matching means) to **distributional matching** (matching the full kernel mean embedding of the population), with applications to post-collection margin adjustment, non-response weighting, panel attrition correction, and nonprobability survey integration.

### 10.1 The Limitations of Classical Calibration

Classical calibration (GREG estimator, raking, post-stratification) finds adjusted weights $w_i^{cal}$ that satisfy linear benchmark constraints:
$$
\sum_{i \in A} w_i^{cal} x_{ij} = T_{x_j} \quad \text{for } j = 1, \ldots, p
$$
where $T_{x_j} = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{U}} x_{ij}$ are the known population totals of $p$ auxiliary variables. The weights are chosen to minimize a distance from the design weights:
$$
\min_{\mathbf{w}^{cal}} \sum_{i \in A} G(w_i^{cal} / w_i) \cdot w_i \quad \text{s.t.} \quad X_A^\top \mathbf{w}^{cal} = \mathbf{T}_x
$$
for a convex distance function $G$ (linear → GREG; multiplicative → raking; logistic → bounded weights).

**Critical limitation:** Classical calibration matches the **first moments** (totals/means) of $p$ auxiliary variables. It provides no guarantee that the **distribution** of the calibrated sample matches the population distribution. Specifically:
1. Matching age/sex/race *totals* does not ensure that the joint distribution of age × income × health status matches the population.
2. For distributional inference — quantile estimation, tail probability estimation, distributional group comparisons — moment-calibrated weights may still produce biased estimators.
3. In high-dimensional settings ($p$ large), the linear calibration system becomes over-determined, requiring regularization that further degrades distributional performance.

The Kullback-Leibler divergence minimization underlying raking implicitly targets the marginal distributions of each variable separately (via iterative proportional fitting), but not their joint distribution.

### 10.2 Distributional Calibration: Kernel Moment Matching

The **distributional calibration problem** replaces the linear moment constraints with a constraint on the full kernel mean embedding:
$$
\min_{\mathbf{w}^{cal} \ge 0,\; \sum w_i^{cal} = \hat{N}} D(\mathbf{w}^{cal}, \mathbf{w}) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \left\|\hat{\mu}_{w^{cal}} - \hat{\mu}_{pop}\right\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 \le \varepsilon
$$
where $\hat{\mu}_{w^{cal}} = \frac{1}{\hat{N}}\sum_{i \in A} w_i^{cal} \phi(X_i)$ is the calibrated kernel mean embedding and $\hat{\mu}_{pop}$ is the population embedding estimated from a census, administrative record, or large reference survey.

Expanding the RKHS norm constraint:
$$
\left\|\hat{\mu}_{w^{cal}} - \hat{\mu}_{pop}\right\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 = \frac{\mathbf{w}^{cal\top} K_{AA} \mathbf{w}^{cal}}{\hat{N}^2} - \frac{2\,\mathbf{k}_{pop}^\top \mathbf{w}^{cal}}{\hat{N}} + c_{pop}
$$
where $K_{AA} = [k(X_i, X_j)]_{i,j \in A}$ is the sample kernel matrix, $\mathbf{k}_{pop} = [\frac{1}{N}\sum_{u \in \mathcal{U}} k(X_u, X_i)]_{i \in A}$ are the cross-kernel evaluations between the population and the sample, and $c_{pop} = \|\hat{\mu}_{pop}\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2$ is a constant.

This reduces to a **quadratic program (QP)** in $\mathbf{w}^{cal}$:
$$
\min_{\mathbf{w}^{cal}} \; \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{w}^{cal\top} K_{AA} \mathbf{w}^{cal} - \hat{N}\, \mathbf{k}_{pop}^\top \mathbf{w}^{cal} \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \mathbf{w}^{cal} \ge 0,\; \mathbf{1}^\top \mathbf{w}^{cal} = \hat{N},\; \mathbf{w}^{cal} \le C \cdot \mathbf{w}
$$
The last constraint bounds the calibrated weights by $C$ times the design weights (preventing extreme weight inflation). This is solvable at scale using `OSQP` (Stellato et al., 2020) or random Fourier feature approximations that reduce the $O(n^2)$ kernel matrix to $O(nD)$ with $D \ll n$.

**Relation to HT-MMD:** The HT-MMD $\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_w$ is the objective function that the distributional calibration minimizes. After calibration, applying the HT-MMD to test $H_0: P_{calibrated} = P_{population}$ serves as the formal **calibration quality diagnostic**: if the test fails to reject, the distributional calibration has successfully closed the gap between the sample and population distributions.

### 10.3 Non-Response Weight Calibration

Non-response is the most pressing source of bias in modern surveys. Response rates for major U.S. surveys have declined from ~80% in the 1980s to below 35% today. Standard non-response weighting proceeds by: (1) modeling response propensity $\hat{p}_i = \hat{P}(R_i = 1 | X_i)$ via logistic regression, (2) constructing non-response adjusted weights $w_i^{nr} = w_i / \hat{p}_i$, and (3) calibrating $w_i^{nr}$ to population totals.

**The limitation.** Propensity score weighting with a logistic model captures only the linear main effects of $X_i$ on response probability. Non-response that depends on complex interactions (e.g., high-income, elderly, rural respondents disproportionately refusing) is not adequately corrected by first-moment calibration.

**Kernel non-response calibration.** The distributional calibration framework directly addresses this. Let $\mathcal{A}_{resp} \subset A$ be the respondent set. The non-response calibrated weights solve:
$$
\min_{\mathbf{w}^{nr} \ge 0} D(\mathbf{w}^{nr}, \mathbf{w}) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \left\|\hat{\mu}_{w^{nr}}^{resp} - \hat{\mu}_{w}^{sample}\right\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 \le \varepsilon
$$
where $\hat{\mu}_{w^{nr}}^{resp} = \frac{1}{\hat{N}_{resp}}\sum_{i \in \mathcal{A}_{resp}} w_i^{nr} \phi(X_i)$ is the respondent kernel embedding and $\hat{\mu}_{w}^{sample}$ is the full-sample HT embedding. This forces the respondent distribution to match the full sample distribution in the RKHS — a much stronger condition than matching age/sex/race totals.

**Testing MAR via HT-MMD.** The Missing at Random (MAR) assumption — $R_i \perp Y_i | X_i$ — is the key identifying assumption for non-response weighting. While MAR is untestable in general (since $Y_i$ for non-respondents is unobserved), the HT-MMD can test a *necessary* condition for MAR: the distribution of the observed auxiliary variables $X_i$ among respondents should match the distribution among non-respondents (up to the design weights). Formally:
$$
H_0^{MAR}: P^{respondents}(X) = P^{non-respondents}(X)
$$
A significant HT-MMD here indicates that respondents and non-respondents differ in their covariate distribution — a strong signal that standard propensity weighting is insufficient and that the stronger kernel calibration is needed.

### 10.4 Raking and Iterative Kernel Calibration

Classical **raking** (iterative proportional fitting, IPF) iteratively calibrates to each auxiliary variable's marginal distribution, cycling until convergence. In the kernel framework, raking generalizes to **iterative kernel calibration** (IKC):

For $t = 1, 2, \ldots$ until convergence:
$$
\mathbf{w}^{(t)} \leftarrow \arg\min_{\mathbf{w} \ge 0} \left\|\hat{\mu}_w^{(j_t)} - \hat{\mu}_{pop}^{(j_t)}\right\|_{\mathcal{H}_{j_t}}^2
$$
where $j_t = (t \bmod p) + 1$ cycles through $p$ covariate groups and $\mathcal{H}_{j_t}$ is the RKHS for the $j_t$-th group's covariate distribution.

By iteratively projecting the calibrated embedding onto the population embedding for each covariate group, IKC converges to weights that match all covariate distributions simultaneously — a nonparametric analogue of raking that does not require specifying marginal categories.

**Convergence:** Under mild conditions on the kernels (bounded, characteristic), the IKC algorithm converges at rate $O(1/t)$ in the RKHS metric $\|\hat{\mu}_w - \hat{\mu}_{pop}\|_\mathcal{H}^2$, matching the convergence rate of classical raking.

### 10.5 Longitudinal Attrition Calibration

In panel surveys, unit attrition — respondents dropping out over successive waves — creates a progressive selection bias. By wave $T$, the remaining panel members systematically differ from wave-1 respondents on health, income, and geographic stability. Standard attrition weighting post-stratifies survivors to wave-1 totals on a few observed characteristics.

**Kernel attrition calibration.** At wave $t$, the attrition-calibrated weights solve:
$$
\min_{\mathbf{w}^{att}} D(\mathbf{w}^{att}, \mathbf{w}^{(t-1)}) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \left\|\hat{\mu}_{w^{att}}^{wave\ t} - \hat{\mu}_{w}^{wave\ 1}\right\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 \le \varepsilon
$$
This forces the distribution of wave-$t$ survivors (under calibrated weights) to match the distribution of wave-1 respondents in the full covariate RKHS — not just on a few summary statistics. Combined with the longitudinal bootstrap calibration from Synthesis I (Section 9), this provides a comprehensive framework for design-corrected inference in panels with informative attrition.

**The HT-MMD attrition test.** Apply the HT-MMD between wave-1 respondents and wave-$t$ respondents to formally test whether attrition has induced distributional shift:
$$
H_0: P_{wave\ 1}(X) = P_{wave\ t}(X)
$$
The test automatically accounts for the complex design through HT weighting and provides a p-value for the significance of cumulative attrition bias — a quantity that survey analysts currently have no principled way to compute.

### 10.6 Nonprobability Survey Integration

The proliferation of opt-in web panels, social media surveys, and big data sources creates a fundamental challenge: these datasets have no known inclusion probabilities and no design-based weights. Yet they often have very large sample sizes and capture variables unavailable in probability surveys.

**The dual-frame problem.** Given a small probability sample $A$ (with HT weights $w_i$) and a large nonprobability sample $B$ (no weights), the goal is to calibrate weights $v_l$ for the nonprobability sample so that it represents the target population.

**Kernel integration calibration.** The calibrated nonprobability weights solve:
$$
\min_{\mathbf{v} \ge 0} D(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{1}) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \left\|\hat{\mu}_v^{B} - \hat{\mu}_w^{A}\right\|_{\mathcal{H}}^2 \le \varepsilon
$$
This forces the distribution of the nonprobability sample (under calibrated weights) to match the distribution of the probability sample — using the probability sample as a reference whose distribution is design-consistently estimated via the HT embedding.

After calibration, the HT-MMD between the calibrated nonprobability sample and the probability sample tests whether the integration was successful:
$$
H_0: P^{B,calibrated}(X) = P^{A,HT}(X)
$$
If not rejected, the calibrated nonprobability weights are distributionally valid and can be used for outcome estimation. If rejected, the nonprobability sample has systematic covariate distributions that cannot be corrected by any weighting (e.g., selection on unobservables).

**Comparison to existing methods.** Existing nonprobability integration methods (Rivers & Bailey, 2009; Wang et al., 2015; Valliant & Dever, 2011) use propensity score matching or post-stratification on a few categories. The kernel integration calibration uses the full multivariate covariate distribution, is applicable in high dimensions, and provides a formal post-calibration diagnostic via the HT-MMD.

### 10.7 Unified Calibration Taxonomy

| Calibration Problem | Constraint Type | Classical Solution | Kernel Solution | HT-MMD Diagnostic |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Post-collection margin adjustment** | Population totals $T_x$ | GREG / raking | Kernel QP to match $\hat{\mu}_{pop}$ | Test calibrated vs. population embedding |
| **Non-response weighting** | Respondent = full sample | Propensity logistic regression | Kernel respondent calibration QP | Test MAR: $P^{resp}(X) = P^{nonresp}(X)$ |
| **Iterative raking** | Marginal distributions | IPF | Iterative kernel calibration (IKC) | Test convergence: $\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2_w < \varepsilon$ |
| **Panel attrition** | Wave $t$ = wave 1 distribution | Post-stratification | Longitudinal kernel attrition calibration | Test attrition shift across waves |
| **Nonprobability integration** | Nonprobability = probability sample | Post-stratification / PSM | Kernel integration calibration QP | Test calibrated nonprobability vs. probability |

The common structure across all five problems is: **(1)** formulate a calibration objective as MMD minimization in the RKHS, **(2)** solve the resulting QP for calibrated weights, **(3)** apply the HT-MMD as a post-calibration diagnostic. This unified framework — calibration as MMD minimization, validation as MMD testing — is a complete, self-consistent methodology that does not currently exist in the survey statistics literature.

## 13. Conclusion

This synthesis establishes that the Maximum Mean Discrepancy — the ML community's canonical distributional test — can be rigorously extended to complex survey data through the HT-weighted MMD. The three pillars of this extension are:

1. **Design-unbiased kernel embeddings** via the Horvitz–Thompson principle applied in the RKHS, correcting the systematic bias of naïve kernel mean estimates under informative sampling.
2. **Correct null distribution** via the HT empirical process theory of Han and Wellner (2021), which replaces the i.i.d. eigenvalues $\lambda_j$ with the design-specific eigenvalues $\lambda_j^\pi$ that reflect the clustering and stratification of the survey.
3. **Bootstrap calibration** via Kim et al.'s (2024) multinomial weight mechanism, which consistently estimates $\lambda_j^\pi$ through the leave-one-out U-statistic structure implicit in the bootstrap.

The HT-MMD opens a broad methodological frontier: covariate shift detection, domain adaptation, fairness auditing, and generative model evaluation can now be conducted on complex survey microdata with the same formal validity guarantees that the survey statistics community requires and that the ML community has been unable to provide. Combined with the DPMM synthetic population framework of Synthesis I, it creates a coherent, two-contribution dissertation that simultaneously advances nonparametric Bayesian methods, design-based asymptotic theory, and practical machine learning tooling for the analysis of survey data.

The population of the United States — observed through its surveys — deserves statistical methods as sophisticated as the designs used to collect it.
